# What we learned

## Know your goals

Before installing solar panels, we didn't know much and just had two goals:

- Be able to power our house during a power outage.
- Reduce our electric bills.

That seemed straight-forward and simple, but after installing solar panels and learning more, our desires and goals changed:

- It's thrilling to see the ridiculous surplus of energy generated in spring and summer!
- Instead of reducing our electric bills in half, they're reduced by 90%--we're so close to being off-grid!
- Let's convert more gas appliances to electric to use all this free excess energy!

And then some truths became apparent:

- Winter sun is so low and cloudy and generates about one-third of summer sun...
- We need a 2nd battery to run the clothes dryer (or multiple appliances) without drawing from the grid...
- We need a few more solar panels ($600) to generate more power to avoid drawing from the grid...
- No installer will do $600 worth of work since they focus on installing $28,000 solar systems...

So, now we're stuck with what we have. We still love our solar panels, but it would've been nice to have a slightly bigger system from the start.

The moral: think carefully what you want to achieve because you can't change your system unless you make super pricy changes that will make it worth the installer's time.

Other things to consider:

- Panels last many decades--at least 40 or 50 years.
- Panels degrade 0.5% per year, so by year 40, your system will generate 20% less than their first year.
- Electricity rates keep increasing, so it's best to oversize your solar system as much as you can.
- Batteries are expensive and can be added later, but start with at least one battery, two if you can.
- 10-year financing is probably the most affordable method (about $260/mo for 8.5 kW + two Powerwalls) and no penalty for extra payments.

## Install as much as you can afford

Solar generation fluctuates throughout the year, so summer months generate 2.5x as much energy as winter months.

- This creates a surplus of energy during summer months that is either stored in batteries or sold to the grid.
- This creates a deficit of energy during winter months that draws power from the grid.
- This is concerning because power outages during the winter are susceptible to having no power, even with solar.

The solution is to buy as many solar panels and batteries as you can afford.

Solar companies recommend you install the amount of solar panels based on how many kWh you used in the last 12 months, as shown on your electric bills. This is a problem because that aims to match your electric bill dollar amount with the minimum number of solar panels. This method relies on the grid heavily during months that you least want an outage to occur.

For about 2-3% more money, you can buy more panels that will heavily reduce your risk of being powerless during an outage.
